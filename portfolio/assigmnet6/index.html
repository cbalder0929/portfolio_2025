“I Think, Therefore I Am.”

A quote famously coined by one of the greatest minds to ever study philosophy and psychology: René Descartes. But who was this man, and why was his work so influential? In this essay, we’ll explore Descartes' life, his philosophical teachings, and the impact of his ideas. I’ll also share my thoughts on why his work still resonates today.

Who Was René Descartes?

Before diving into the depths of his philosophy, it’s important to understand who Descartes was. René Descartes (pronounced Dee-Karr) was a French philosopher and mathematician who lived from 1596 to 1650. Although born in France, he spent much of his adult life in the Netherlands, where he served in the Dutch Army. Descartes came from a prominent family—his father was a member of parliament, while his mother tragically died shortly after giving birth to one of his siblings.

One thing that stood out to me about Descartes is that he was a Roman Catholic, which I found relatable since I am also Catholic. This small connection made me feel a bit more drawn to his work. It’s interesting to think that despite living centuries apart, we share the same faith.

Descartes' Philosophy: The Foundation of Dualism

At the core of Descartes’ teachings is the concept of dualism—the belief that reality consists of two fundamental substances: the physical (body) and the non-physical (mind or soul). According to Descartes, humans are made up of both. The body is responsible for our physical existence, while the mind (or consciousness) is where our thoughts, emotions, and self-awareness reside.

This dualism also applied to his famous concept of existence. Descartes argued that thinking and existing are interconnected—you can only be certain of your own existence through the act of thinking. Hence, his famous statement:

"I think, therefore I am."

To Descartes, the very act of doubting or questioning reality was proof of his own existence.

I also found his view on God fascinating. Descartes believed that God is the ultimate "thinker"—the observer and creator of all existence. In his reasoning, the mere fact that we are capable of imagining a perfect being (God) is, in itself, evidence of God’s existence. This is because the concept of perfection, he argued, could not originate from imperfect beings like us.

The Impact of Descartes' Work

What I find most remarkable about Descartes is how much of modern thought stems from his ideas. He laid the groundwork for scientific rationalism, promoting the use of logic, reason, and evidence to understand the world—an approach that heavily influenced the Enlightenment Era. This was a time of immense intellectual and cultural change when society began shifting away from superstition and toward reason, science, and individualism.

To me, this era seems both fascinating and overwhelming. It must have felt like a revolutionary time—almost apocalyptic—when people were suddenly becoming more aware of themselves and the world around them. This self-awareness was likely both liberating and terrifying. It’s interesting to imagine what it must have been like to live during that period when centuries-old beliefs were being challenged.

My Thoughts on Descartes' Influence

What I admire about Descartes is his commitment to objective reasoning. He sought to understand the world based on irrefutable truths rather than mere assumptions. His belief that it is impossible to distinguish between dreaming and reality was a profound realization. It made me think about how we, as humans, are constantly aware of our own thoughts. Even when we dream, we are still thinking—proof that we are indeed "something" experiencing "something" in a place, even if that place is uncertain.

I also wonder why Descartes was so driven to seek the truth. It’s possible that his wealth and privilege gave him the luxury to pursue knowledge. He had access to education, fine clothing, and companionship. But perhaps, after having all the material comforts, he felt unfulfilled and was driven to seek something deeper—the truth of existence itself.

What makes Descartes even more influential is how relatable his ideas are. We’ve all questioned reality at some point—whether during a vivid dream or while contemplating life’s purpose. His philosophy gave people a toolset for rational thinking—a way to tame the more primitive, instinctual parts of human nature. During his time, people were driven by superstition and emotion, which often led to irrational decisions. Descartes’ emphasis on logic offered a new framework for making decisions based on reason rather than impulse.

The Duality of Self-Awareness

While I admire Descartes' promotion of reason, I also find it interesting that self-awareness comes with its own dangers. As people became more self-aware, they also became more destructive. The warlords and conquerors of the Enlightenment era, for example, may have been driven by newfound knowledge and ambition, yet they used it for violence and domination. This raises an interesting question:

Were we better off being unaware?

While Descartes argued for the pursuit of knowledge and self-awareness, it’s clear that consciousness can lead to both enlightenment and chaos. However, despite the risks, I believe the benefits of self-awareness outweigh the dangers. The progress made in science, philosophy, and human rights during and after Descartes’ time shows how powerful rational thinking can be.

Conclusion

René Descartes was far more than a philosopher—he was a pioneer of reason and self-awareness. His ideas about dualism, existence, and rationalism shaped the way we think and live today. Through his pursuit of undeniable truths, he gave humanity a framework for questioning reality and seeking knowledge. Though the Enlightenment had its dark side, the overall impact of Descartes' philosophy was positive—it helped humanity become more thoughtful, rational, and self-aware.

His legacy teaches us that questioning the world around us is not only natural but necessary. It is through this process that we continue to grow, both individually and collectively.
